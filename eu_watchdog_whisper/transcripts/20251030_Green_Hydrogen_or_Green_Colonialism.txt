Hello and welcome! I'm Isabela Franco from Counterbalance and you are listening to EU Watchdog Radio. This is the new season of our monthly podcast and with it you will hear some new voices. I'm the new Communication and Advocacy intern and my colleague Laura Verhek has joined us as the new Policy Officer at Counterbalance. Across Europe, the debate around green hydrogen is growing fast. Clean hydrogen is a perfect means towards our goal of climate neutrality. It can power heavy industries, propel our cars or trucks and our planes. It can store seasonal energy or heat up our homes. And all of this with almost zero emissions. Those are the beautiful promise of Ursula von der Leyen, the President of the European Commission. But so far we haven't proven that we can really use hydrogen for all those things. But what we know is that we can transport hydrogen using gas pipelines and continue being dependent on fossil fuels industry. And the gas industry loves this. In today's episode Laura speaks with Hikma Bashagur, who is an Assistant Professor in the CD Mohammed Ben Abdel University in Fez, Morocco. And together with the MENA FEM movement wrote a powerful report about the development of the green hydrogen sector in Morocco. And how it could affect vulnerable communities, women and gender minorities, who are too often left out the decision making process. Let's get into it. Across Africa, large scale renewable energy projects such as hydropower, solar and biofuels are often supported by European policies and investments. Yet many of these projects have been criticized for reinforcing structural imbalances in energy governance, serving European interests, while undermining African energy sovereignty. How do you view this dynamic, especially in the Moroccan context? This is a very important question because when we look at the energy projects across Africa, we can feel that the cooperation between Europe and African countries is not always balanced. There is still this background feeling that Europe benefits the most from Africa's natural and renewable resources. So many projects are shaped to answer Europe's energy needs first. For example, its plan to decarbonize and secure clean energy supplies, especially in the framework of the Green Deal. And only later do we talk about how they can help African countries. So in that sense, we cannot really say that the cooperation is already a fair win-win model. It has a lot of potential, but the benefits are not equally shared yet. And this is also where the global gateway comes in. It is presented as a new way for the European Union to build sustainable and fair partnerships with Africa and the global South. But many in the region are still wondering, will this really be different or will it repeat the same patterns as before? So because when we look at the language, we see good intentions, green, inclusive digital connectivity. But we are still waiting to see how those ideas translate into shared governance, local value and real ownership for partner countries. So in Morocco, we are proud to be a trusted partner for Europe. And that can be a good thing. But we need to make sure that this partnership also supports local industries training and job creation, not only energy exports. So since 2009, under the enlightened vision of His Majesty King Mohammed VI, Morocco made the clear choice to put clean energy at the heart of its development model. This choice was never about dependency. It was about building our own sovereignty and competitiveness through renewable energy. What we need today is a more balanced partnership, one that combines European demand with Moroccan industrial development, innovation and local prosperity. Because cooperation cannot be called green if all the value stays on one side. A true partnership is when both sides grow and benefit together. As you mentioned in the report, many renewable energy projects in Morocco are financed by international institutions like the World Bank or the European Investment Bank. However, this financing often comes in the form of loans, which can create debt and dependency. From your perspective, how does this model of financing affect Morocco's energy sovereignty and the well-being of local communities? This is also a key question. Most renewable projects in developing countries, including in Morocco, they are financed mainly by loans, not by grants. And even if these projects are sustainable, loans increase public debt. So this creates a kind of financial pressure, because even when the project is successful, part of the value goes back to the lenders and the companies that supplied the technology. It's not only a Moroccan issue. This is how the international financing system is structured for the Global South. The challenge is how to make these investments more fair. That means including local development goals from the very beginning, not later as small social projects. So if every renewable project also invests in skills, infrastructure and community benefits, it can become a real driver of growth. Morocco is already moving in this direction by linking climate finance to regional development. For example, the regions where solar and wind farms are located should also get better services, training programs and opportunities for youth. That's how we make the transition both green and just. Morocco is being positioned as a major producer of green hydroten. How do you see the implication of this model for Morocco, especially when such a large share of production is planned for export? Morocco's green hydrogen plan is very ambitious. But as you said, at this stage, most of the production is planned for export to Europe, since that's where the current demand is. That can be positive if it helps us build strong industries at home. Hydrogen is not only about exporting molecules, it's about creating new industries, jobs and skills. It's about developing infrastructures like ports, storage and transport that can also serve our own economy. What we want to avoid is a model where hydrogen becomes another raw material that leaves the country without transformation or local value. That's why it's important to keep parts of this hydrogen production for domestic use. It's also important to make sure that European partners work with us in a spirit of co-development, not just in a buyer-supplier logic. And here again, the global gateway can be an opportunity if it moves beyond investment numbers and focuses on fair benefit sharing and technology transfer. If Morocco becomes a main partner for Europe under this new framework, it should also mean stronger regional integration, industrial development and resilience at home. Being a main partner for Europe is a good opportunity as long as the benefits are shared fairly. This includes sharing knowledge, creating jobs and making Morocco stronger in the long term. After hearing directly from feminist and local organizations in Morocco about the harms caused by renewable energy projects, how do you see the EU's push for green hydrogen exports, which will demand even more renewable energy and water, impacting those same communities? When we talk with local communities, many say they have already seen this story before with other renewable projects. They are told that new projects will bring jobs and opportunities, but very often they are not really consulted and they don't see direct benefits. In some areas, especially those that are already facing water scarcity, people are worried. Hydrogen production needs a lot of water. And the question is, who will get priority, the export projects or the local farmers? To make sure this transition is fair, we must involve communities from the very beginning, not after decisions are already made. We also need to guarantee that local people, especially women and young people, get real opportunities. Morocco has made progress in public participation, but it needs to be applied systematically as hydrogen projects grow. This is also where our European partners can make a difference by making sure that social inclusion and transparency are seriously considered in the projects they finance. When we do that, hydrogen can be not only clean, but also just and inclusive. As we already bring to the discussion, Morocco's green hydrogen strategy overlooks issues of gender and social justice. From your experience, how women and marginalised groups being affected are left out in current discussions and plans around hydrogen? I would like to clarify one thing. It's not really Morocco's national strategy that forgets about gender and social justice. The real gap comes from how international financing and cooperation frameworks are designed. They often focus on exports, capacity and technology and forget the social and gender aspects. Inside Morocco, we have seen real progress. Women are taking more leadership roles in environmental and energy sectors. A great example is our Minister of Energy, Laila Ben Ali, a woman leading one of the most strategic portfolios in the country. Her work shows that inclusion and excellence can go together. But these advances are not always reflected in international projects. Too often women are still left out of consultation processes and job opportunities linked to large energy projects. In our research with MENAFE movement, we found that when women and youth are included, not just as beneficiaries but as active partners, projects become stronger and more accepted by communities. So the message is simple. A just transition must also be an inclusive transition. Europe and Morocco both have a role to play in making sure that climate investments create equal opportunities for all. After recording this podcast with Iqma, we co-organized an event where she spoke to journalists. There's a very important thing that she says there that I'd like you to listen to. The current European approach risks replicating a kind of green colonialism, where Africa provides the clean energy and raw materials needed for European transition without real local benefits. So from my conversations with these grassroots organizations, people are not against renewable energy projects. But what they need is projects that brings, creates jobs that preserves water and other natural resources. And also that involves women and local communities in the planning and governance. And I believe that if the global gateway is really serious about fairness, it should apply the same principles we discuss in climate diplomacy. And I'm talking specifically about the principle of common but differentiated responsibilities, because Europe as a historical amateur and wealthy bloc has a responsibility to offer grants to ensure technology transfer and capacity building and not adding more debt in African countries. Because once again, just transition cannot be imported from Brussels or Washington. It must be co-created and it must be co-created with people who live closest to the land and people who will bear the consequences of these projects. Thank you for listening. I am Joana from CEO, Corp. Europe Observatory, and together with Counterbalance we produced this podcast. We hope you enjoyed it.
